# Systems Thinking: Structure Drives Behavior (Video 3 of 5)

0:00 Systems have distinct behaviors that are determined by the structure.
0:04 To put it simply, the characteristics of the system, or the structure, drives its behavior.
0:10 Let's look at classroom setup as an example.
0:13 If a teacher wants students to watch them lecture and maybe take some notes,
0:17 they should arrange desks into rows so that all students are facing the front, with few distractions.
0:23 But if a teacher wants students to collaborate and work together,
0:26 They might group desks together to make a huddle or table-like structure that encourages discussion and sharing.
0:33 How you structure the room and the desks will affect the student's actions.
0:38 Unlike the rules of a game, which are typically included in a package or available online,
0:43 most of the systems we work with don't come with clear and concise printed instructions.
0:47 Structures can be present in written or unwritten policies and procedures,
0:51 social or organizational norms, power structures and dynamics,
0:56 or through distribution of resources and institutionalized ways of working.
1:00 Looking at the structure helps us analyze and begin to understand the ways in which the structure drives behavior.
1:08 Using systems thinking, we are able to see not just what is happening, but also why it's happening.
