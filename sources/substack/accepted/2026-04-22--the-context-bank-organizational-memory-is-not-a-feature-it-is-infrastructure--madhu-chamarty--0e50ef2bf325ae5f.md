# The Context Bank: Organizational Memory Is Not a Feature. It Is Infrastructure.

## Verified source excerpt

In 2005, fresh out of grad school, I joined Keystone.ai as a consulting analyst. Keystone at the time was a young boutique strategy and economics firm co-founded by [Marco Iansiti](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/profile.aspx?facId=6482), the David Sarnoff Professor of Business Administration at Harvard Business School, alongside former students and colleagues. One of my first projects was run by Marco on behalf of a major aerospace prime, the kind of company that builds systems so large and so complex that the government does not just buy the product, it buys the institutional knowledge required to sustain it. The client was a lead systems integrator on a significant defense modernization initiative, and the question on the table was this: if the Department of Defense decided to move away from them, what would actually be lost?

The answer was not planes or factories or patents. Those were recoverable. What was at risk was something harder to name and nearly impossible to transfer: decades of accumulated organizational understanding about how to make a system of systems actually work. Every workaround, every undocumented interface decision, every lesson encoded into process after a failure nobody wanted to repeat. A living memory of how to build those things safely. And that memory lived inside the organization in a way that could not be extracted, documented, or handed off.

One of the frameworks we reached for was Eric von Hippel’s sticky information. Von Hippel, a professor at MIT Sloan and one of the most cited scholars in innovation research, introduced the concept decades ago. The idea is deceptively simple: some knowledge is so embedded in the context that produced it that moving it to where it needs to be used costs more than the knowledge is worth. It sticks. Not because anyone is hoarding it, but because it only makes sense inside the experience that created it.

That idea stayed with me. And it came back with force recently, reading about the race to sell enterprises better context for their AI agents.

> Because what I keep seeing is this: everyone is solving the context delivery problem. Nobody is solving the context ownership problem. And those are not the same problem.

Jensen Huang says every nation needs its own AI. The argument is not complicated: if your intelligence infrastructure runs on someone else’s compute, trained on someone else’s data, optimized for someone else’s strategic interests, you have not achieved sovereignty. You have achieved dependency with better branding.

What strikes me is that this argument applies with equal force one layer down from the nation and one layer up from the model. Every sufficiently large organization is a microcosm of the dynamics that make national sovereignty worth fighting for. It has institutional knowledge that took decades to accumulate. It has competitive differentiation encoded in how it operates, not just what it builds. It has, in von Hippel’s sense, sticky information that cannot be cleanly extracted and handed to a competitor without destroying what makes it valuable.
