# Absorptive capacity and intraorganizational inventor network structures

## Relevance decision

Title directly studies absorptive capacity, an established organizational learning and knowledge-conversion construct.

## Preserved evidence

A firm’s absorptive capacity is delineated by its abilities to sequentially identify, assimilate, and exploit valuable external knowledge. Although numerous inquiries have explored how firms identify and exploit valuable knowledge, relatively little work has explored the assimilation dimension of absorptive capacity, let alone how knowledge assimilation and mechanisms used to identify external knowledge are part of an integrated process. We explore how a firm's internal inventor network structure influences its ability to assimilate and exploit external technology. Specifically, we examine how densely connected inventor network structures influence the absorptive capacity of firms in opposite ways. Using a sample of 38 incumbent firms in the U.S. information technology sector and analyzing their citing behaviors of the patents developed by 370 new IT ventures over the course of sixteen years, we find that incumbent firms having inventor network configurations that were highly clustered were generally more likely to build on and exploit the technologies of our sample new ventures. In contrast, high network connectivity beyond that which contributes to clustering decreased the extent to which our sample incumbent firms exploited the technologies of new ventures. We also find that such high connectivity diminishes any advantage that incumbent firms might gain from forming CVC partnerships in terms of being able to absorb valuable technology from new venture investees. Our findings contribute to research on absorptive capacity, social networks and CVC.
