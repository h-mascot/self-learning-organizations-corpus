Title: The Context Bank: Organizational Memory Is Not a Feature. It Is Infrastructure.

Published Time: 2026-04-22T07:50:04+00:00

In 2005, fresh out of grad school, I joined Keystone.ai as a consulting analyst. Keystone at the time was a young boutique strategy and economics firm co-founded by [Marco Iansiti](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/profile.aspx?facId=6482), the David Sarnoff Professor of Business Administration at Harvard Business School, alongside former students and colleagues. One of my first projects was run by Marco on behalf of a major aerospace prime, the kind of company that builds systems so large and so complex that the government does not just buy the product, it buys the institutional knowledge required to sustain it. The client was a lead systems integrator on a significant defense modernization initiative, and the question on the table was this: if the Department of Defense decided to move away from them, what would actually be lost?

_The shelves are empty. The city is above. The light is leaving._

The answer was not planes or factories or patents. Those were recoverable. What was at risk was something harder to name and nearly impossible to transfer: decades of accumulated organizational understanding about how to make a system of systems actually work. Every workaround, every undocumented interface decision, every lesson encoded into process after a failure nobody wanted to repeat. A living memory of how to build those things safely. And that memory lived inside the organization in a way that could not be extracted, documented, or handed off.

One of the frameworks we reached for was Eric von Hippel’s sticky information. Von Hippel, a professor at MIT Sloan and one of the most cited scholars in innovation research, introduced the concept decades ago. The idea is deceptively simple: some knowledge is so embedded in the context that produced it that moving it to where it needs to be used costs more than the knowledge is worth. It sticks. Not because anyone is hoarding it, but because it only makes sense inside the experience that created it.

That idea stayed with me. And it came back with force recently, reading about the race to sell enterprises better context for their AI agents.

> Because what I keep seeing is this: everyone is solving the context delivery problem. Nobody is solving the context ownership problem. And those are not the same problem.

Jensen Huang says every nation needs its own AI. The argument is not complicated: if your intelligence infrastructure runs on someone else’s compute, trained on someone else’s data, optimized for someone else’s strategic interests, you have not achieved sovereignty. You have achieved dependency with better branding.

What strikes me is that this argument applies with equal force one layer down from the nation and one layer up from the model. Every sufficiently large organization is a microcosm of the dynamics that make national sovereignty worth fighting for. It has institutional knowledge that took decades to accumulate. It has competitive differentiation encoded in how it operates, not just what it builds. It has, in von Hippel’s sense, sticky information that cannot be cleanly extracted and handed to a competitor without destroying what makes it valuable.

And right now, as organizations deploy AI agents at scale, that organizational sovereignty is quietly eroding. Not through any hostile act. Through an architectural default nobody has questioned.

The default is this: organizational context flows to vendors rather than accumulating in an enterprise-owned layer. Every context platform, every agent vendor, every process intelligence tool that enters the enterprise takes operational exhaust and uses it to make their product more accurate and more defensible. The organization receives a better service. The vendor accumulates a more durable asset.

The organization receives a service. The vendor receives an asset.

This is different from the systems of record enterprises have always used. Workday stores what happened. ServiceNow logs what was requested. Those systems hold your data as a passive repository. The structural leak is about something newer: platforms that deploy active intelligence into your organization and improve across every customer by learning how each one operates. The prior generation stored your exhaust. This generation trains on it.

So this asymmetry is not new. What is new is the scale and irreversibility that AI agents introduce. And that changes everything about whether the asymmetry is tolerable.

Every organization continuously generates what I am going to call context exhaust. It is the byproduct of every decision, exception, workaround, and institutional settlement that happens across the org on any given day. Some of it lands in systems of record. Most of it does not.

The gap between what your org knows and what your systems contain is not a documentation failure. It is a structural property of how organizational knowledge forms. Von Hippel’s sticky information captures it precisely: knowledge that is costly to transfer, resistant to capture, and shaped by the context that produced it. The reason it does not get written down is not laziness. It is that the knowledge only makes sense inside the experience that created it.

A vendor routed through secondary approval is trivial on paper. In practice, it encodes a six-week supply chain disruption, a set of legal proceedings that went unresolved, and an institutional caution that lived in the memory of one procurement lead and propagated through organizational behavior rather than policy documentation. You cannot extract that from a log. You cannot derive it from an invoice record. It is path-dependent knowledge, forged through consequence, and it is irreversible once lost.

Organizations have always leaked this. The question is whether the leak rate matters. Until now, it mostly did not.

Agents do not create this problem. They remove the buffer that previously hid it.

Human workers absorb organizational context through immersion. Not quickly, and certainly not completely, but enough to operate with coherence. An agent starts from zero every time. It does not passively accumulate tacit understanding. It does not notice the unwritten rules. It negotiates with the confidence of someone who has read the policy manual, which is to say with no caution at all about the things the policy manual could not possibly contain.

At one agent, this is manageable. At a thousand agents operating simultaneously across procurement, finance, customer success, and legal, it is a systematic erosion of organizational intelligence at a scale no human workforce could accidentally achieve.

The first organizations to run agents at scale without owning their context will unknowingly export their operating intelligence into the market. By the time they recognize the loss, the asset will already belong to someone else.

And the supply-side problem compounds the demand-side one. Every agent vendor, every context platform, every process intelligence tool that enters the enterprise takes organizational exhaust and uses it to make their product smarter. The vendor’s model improves across every customer it touches. The organization resets with every new deployment. The vendor’s moat deepens. The organization’s sovereign advantage diffuses.

This is the structural leak. Organizational context does not disappear when vendors touch it. It leaks predictably, structurally, and at the point of integration with every external system. The vendor instruments all of this to make their product more accurate. This is what good vendors do. The problem is not their intent. The problem is the architectural default that makes this the only available path. Once lost, this context is not recoverable from any system of record. It is not missing data. It is destroyed organizational knowledge.

There is no RFP category for context sovereignty. There is no vendor to call. There is no standard for what it would mean to own your organizational context as an independent, compounding asset.

The absence of vocabulary is itself the problem. You cannot protect what you have not named.

I spend a lot of time at the intersection of enterprise AI deployment and the infrastructure layer underneath it, and what I see is a market that has correctly identified the context problem and is solving the wrong half of it.

There are excellent companies building what I would call context feeds: platforms that extract organizational knowledge, structure it, and deliver the right context to the right agent at the right moment. They are solving a real problem. WorkFabric, backed by a substantial Cognizant partnership, is doing this at scale. Edra, freshly funded by Sequoia, is mining operational data to build living playbooks. Glean, at $7.2 billion in valuation and $200 million in ARR, is building what they call a system of context for organizational intelligence.

These are serious companies doing serious work. Each has made the architectural choice to optimize for context delivery rather than context ownership, and that choice is correct for their business model. It also means the ownership problem remains structurally unaddressed. A company running any of these platforms still has no sovereign, compounding context layer it owns independently. The feed gets smarter. The bank does not get built.

Nobody is building the locker.

The locker is what I am calling the Context Bank: a sovereign, enterprise-owned memory layer that the organization builds, controls, and compounds independently of any agent vendor. The bank does not replace existing systems of record. It sits alongside them, capturing and structuring the context those systems cannot represent. Each interaction increases the coverage, validation, and reliability of what the organization knows about itself. The compounding value accrues to the organization, not to the vendor.

Feed is a service. Bank is an asset.

_Same street. Different philosophy._

This does not mean vendors become irrelevant. It means the relationship changes shape. Today a vendor improves by training on your exhaust because there is no alternative architecture. With a context bank in place, a vendor's agent connects to your bank through a permissioned API, draws the context it needs to do its job well, and deposits what it learns back into your bank as part of the service agreement. You define what gets read, what gets written, and what stays private. The vendor gets higher quality context than it could mine on its own. You get a smarter service plus a compounding asset you own. Think of it the way modern data infrastructure works: Snowflake stores the data, Salesforce reads what it needs, the company controls what gets shared. Nobody conflates storage ownership with access denial. The context bank works the same way. Sovereign does not mean closed. It means you set the terms.

This is not a subtle distinction. It is the difference between renting intelligence and owning it. Between organizational sovereignty and organizational dependency. Between a context layer that gets smarter for the vendor and one that gets smarter for you.

Here is where I want to get specific, because the architectural argument matters and it is the part most people will skip.

The reason no one has built the locker is not that the need is unclear. It is that the right data primitive does not exist yet. Existing database categories were built for different objects.

A log captures what happened. A document captures what was written. A vector embedding captures semantic similarity. A graph node captures relationships. None of these capture what an agent understood, at what moment, with what confidence, shaped by what organizational history, with what expected shelf life.

This is not a better database problem. It is a missing primitive problem.

I am proposing a new primitive: the Context Object. Not a document, not an event, and not a graph node, but a time-bound, confidence-weighted, provenance-linked unit of organizational understanding whose retrieval behavior is governed by time, confidence, and lineage simultaneously.

Six properties make it categorically distinct from existing primitives:

**Temporal validity with decay functions.** Context has a half-life. A vendor sentiment signal from last week and one from eight months ago are not the same object. A compliance requirement and a customer preference do not age the same way. The primitive needs native decay, not just a timestamp.

**Provenance chains.** Not just where data came from, but what chain of inference produced the understanding. Was this derived from a human decision, an agent synthesis, a system output, or a combination? Downstream agents need to know how much to trust what they are receiving, and that trust is a function of the derivation path.

**Confidence scores.** An agent’s belief about something is not binary. The primitive carries epistemic weight natively, a structured representation of certainty that retrieval systems can act on directly.

**Context grade.** This is the one I think is most original, and it builds directly on an idea I explored in a recent piece called The Friction Fallacy: not all organizational friction is the same thing. Some encodes compliance requirements that cannot be bypassed. Some encodes expired workarounds nobody has cleaned up. Some encodes institutional memory forged through failure, the organizational scar tissue that only surfaces when an agent violates it and something breaks.

Context grade captures the organizational weight a process carries, which is not the same as how much resistance it creates. Think of it like slope: a steep grade is load-bearing, a flat one is inertia, and a complex grade shifts as organizational conditions change. High grade context is Chesterton’s Fence. Low grade context is a cowpath someone forgot to close. An agent that cannot distinguish between them will optimize away the fence and wonder why the cattle escaped.

Classifying which type an agent is encountering changes everything about how it should act.

**Organizational lineage.** How did this understanding come to exist? Was it forged through repeated failure recovery, or copied from a policy document? Context shaped by consequence carries different organizational weight than context derived from documentation alone. This is the von Hippel property, the one that makes sticky information not just describable but retrievable.

**Cross-agent attribution.** Which agents have read this context object, acted on it, and what did they do as a result? This closes the feedback loop and allows the bank to get smarter as agent actions validate or invalidate existing context over time.

These properties can be approximated in existing systems, but only by pushing core semantics into the application layer, which breaks at enterprise scale. Systems optimized for static data and query performance are architecturally misaligned with the requirements of decision-grade context as a primary storage unit. This is not a gap incumbents can close incrementally. Closing it requires building around a different primary unit of storage and retrieval.

The context bank only works if it captures all three layers of organizational reality. Each requires a different instrumentation approach, and each captures something the others miss.

Agent exhaust is the richest stream. Every time an agent acts, it can explicitly deposit what it knew, what it concluded, and what it did. The confidence score comes directly from the model’s own output. The provenance chain is captured in real time. This is the declared stream, and it is the most auditable.

Structured system exhaust is the most accessible. Event logs from enterprise systems capture outcomes, actors, and timestamps. They map cleanly to most context object attributes. The context grade and organizational lineage require inference on top, but the raw material is there.

Behavioral exhaust is the most revealing. Everything that happens between systems, the application switching, the manual reconciliation steps that never land in a system of record, this is where the organizational why lives. It is the hardest to turn into structured context objects, and the most valuable when you do.

Together, these three streams produce a picture of organizational intelligence that no single source could provide: what decisions were made, what work surrounded them, and what the agents operating in that environment understood at the moment they acted.

Workday did not become Workday because it built better HR software. It became Workday because it recognized that the human workforce needed a system of record, a place where every decision about a human worker accumulated, compounded, and remained under organizational control regardless of which individual managers came and went.

What I am describing is that, for the agentic workforce.

Every agent deployed without a context bank is a new hire who gets no onboarding, inherits no institutional memory, and takes the job literally. The organization gets the work done, in the narrow sense. It does not get the organizational intelligence that would make the work compounding rather than merely productive.

And the sovereignty frame closes the loop. Jensen Huang is right that nations need their own AI. What he has not said yet, and what nobody has said yet, is that every organization needs its own context bank. The argument is structurally identical: control the layer where intelligence accumulates, or accept that someone else will. Nations are learning this about compute. Organizations are about to learn it about context.

The system of record for the agentic workforce does not exist yet. The infrastructure to build it has not been named. The primitive that would make it possible has not been formally specified.

This essay is the opening move, not the full argument.

The next piece in this series will describe a synthetic organization simulation I am building to validate the primitive empirically: a controlled environment where agents operate across a series of simulated weeks, depositing context into the bank, and where I can show directly what a deployed agent knows with the bank versus without it. The difference in decision quality, auditability, and organizational coherence is the proof of concept.

After that, a formal technical specification for the Context Object and the Context Bank architecture: the schema, the retrieval semantics, the ingest mechanisms, and the formal argument for why this requires a purpose-built storage primitive rather than a wrapper on existing infrastructure.

The intent is not just to write about this. It is to build it, demonstrate it, and make the case with working code and real results rather than assertions.

It is 12:48am, and I find myself thinking about what organizations are in the process of giving away without knowing it. Not through negligence or bad faith. Through the simple absence of infrastructure that has never had to exist before.

There is a particular kind of loss that only becomes visible in retrospect. Not the loss of a product line or a market position, which show up in the numbers and force a response. But the loss of the understanding that made certain decisions possible, the institutional knowledge that governed how things actually worked rather than how they were documented. That kind of loss is quiet. It accumulates slowly, then all at once, and by the time it is legible it is already irreversible.

What I keep thinking about is that we are at the beginning of a decade in which organizations will deploy more autonomous intelligence than in all of prior history combined. Most will do it without ever building the layer that makes that intelligence sovereign, compounding, and theirs. The ones that figure this out first will not just have better agents. They will have something rarer: an organization that actually learns from its own experience, at scale, over time, without giving that learning away.

That asset does not exist yet. It will.
